# VR Measurement Scales

This document aggregates the standard measurement scales recommended for VR studies. It provides a summary of each scale, its intended use case, and the specific items (questions) that should be administered to participants. For some constructs, we provide multiple measurement scales to choose from. 

## Baseline Measurements:

The Igroup Presence Questionnaire (IPQ) is the most widely used and validated measure of presence in VR studies. It is recommended for all VR studies. We provide the SUS and SPES as alternatives.

### 1. Igroup Presence Questionnaire (IPQ)
- **Purpose:** Measures presence (the feeling of being physically present in the virtually enriched world).
- **Link:** https://www.igroup.org/pq/ipq/items.php
- **Items:** 14
- **Reliability (α):** .87

**Items:**
1. How aware were you of the real world surrounding you while navigating in the virtual world? (i.e. sounds, room temperature, other people, etc.)? [INV – reversed]
2. How real did the virtual world seem to you? [REAL]
3. I had a sense of acting in the virtual space, rather than operating something from outside. [SP]
4. How much did your experience in the virtual environment seem consistent with your real world experience? [REAL]
5. How real did the virtual world seem to you? [GP – single general presence item]
6. I did not feel present in the virtual space. [SP – reversed]
7. I was not aware of my real environment. [INV]
8. In the computer generated world I had a sense of "being there". [SP]
9. Somehow I felt that the virtual world surrounded me. [SP]
10. I felt present in the virtual space. [SP]
11. I still paid attention to the real environment. [INV – reversed]
12. The virtual world seemed more realistic than the real world. [REAL]
13. I felt like I was just perceiving pictures. [REAL – reversed]
14. I was completely captivated by the virtual world. [INV]

**Scoring:**
- **Spatial Presence (SP)** = mean(items 3, 6[R], 8, 9, 10)
- **Involvement (INV)** = mean(items 1[R], 7, 11[R], 14)
- **Experienced Realism (REAL)** = mean(items 2, 4, 12, 13[R])
- **General Presence (GP)** = item 5

*(Note: [R] denotes reverse-scored items)*

### 2. Slater-Usoh-Steed (SUS) Presence
- **Purpose:** Measures presence and the sense of "being there" in the virtual environment.
- **Link:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/2457864_Using_Presence_Questionnaires_in_Reality
- **Items:** 6
- **Reliability (α):** .75

**Items:**
1. Please rate your sense of being in the office space, on the following scale from 1 to 7, where 7 represents your normal experience of being in a place. *"I had a sense of ‘‘being there’’ in the office space."* (1) Not at all. (7) Very much.
2. To what extent were there times during the experience when the office space was the reality for you? *There were times during the experience when the office space was the reality for me...* (1) At no time. (7) Almost all the time.
3. When you think back about your experience, do you think of the office space more as images that
you saw, or more as somewhere that you visited? *The office space seems to me to be more like...* (1) Images that I saw. (7) Somewhere that I visited.
4. During the time of the experience, which was strongest on the whole, your sense of being in the
office space, or of being elsewhere? *I had a stronger sense of...* (1) Being elsewhere. (7) Being in the office space.
5. Consider your memory of being in the office space. How similar in terms of the structure of the
memory is this to the structure of the memory of other places you have been today? By ‘‘structure of
the memory,’’ consider things like the extent to which you have a visual memory of the office space, whether that memory is in color, the extent to which the memory seems vivid or realistic, its size, location in your imagination, the extent to which it is panoramic in your imagination, and other such structural elements. *I think of the office space as a place in a way similar to other places that I’ve been today...* (1) Not at all. (7) Very much so.
6. During the time of the experience, did you often think to yourself that you were actually in the of-
fice space? *During the experience I often thought that I was really standing in the office space...* (1) Not very often. (7) Very much so.

**Scoring:**
Presence score = count of items (out of 6) rated 6 or 7. Higher score = greater presence.

### 3. Spatial Presence Experience Scale (SPES)
- **Purpose:** Measures spatial presence in the virtual environment.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1027/1864-1105/a000137
- **Items:** 8
- **Reliability (α):** .89

**Items:** *(Note: Replace "presentation" with the specific medium or Virtual Environment used in your study)*  
*Self-Location (SL):*  
1. (SL1) I felt like I was actually there in the environment of the presentation.
2. (SL2) It seemed as though I actually took part in the action of the presentation.
3. (SL3) It was as though my true location had shifted into the environment in the presentation.
4. (SL4) I felt as though I was physically present in the environment of the presentation.

*Perceived Possible Actions (PA):*  
1. (PA1) The objects in the presentation gave me the feeling that I could do things with them.  
2. (PA2) I had the impression that I could be active in the environment of the presentation.  
3. (PA3) I felt like I could move around among the objects in the presentation.  
4. (PA4) I felt like I could jump into the action.  

**Scoring:**
Rated on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = I do not agree at all; 5 = I fully agree).
- **Self-Location (SL)** = mean(SL1–SL4)
- **Perceived Possible Actions (PA)** = mean(PA1–PA4)
- **SPES Total** = mean(all 8 items)

We recommend including a measure of VR familiarity in all VR studies. This can be done using the following items:

### 4. VR Familiarity
- **Purpose:** Measures the prior exposure participants have had to VR technology.
- **Items:** 2

**Items:**
1. How familiar are you with VR?
2. How many sessions have you spent in VR?

**Scoring:**
Self-reported on a customized scale (e.g., 1-5 or open-ended for sessions).


## If Locomotion is Used:

When participants are allowed to move within the virtual environment (that is, they are not seated), it is important to measure cybersickness. This can be done using the CSQ-VR scale or the Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ). The CSQ-VR is a more recent scale that has been shown to be more sensitive to cybersickness than the SSQ. The SSQ is a more comprehensive scale that measures a wider range of symptoms.

### 5. Cybersickness in Virtual Reality Questionnaire (CSQ-VR)
- **Purpose:** Used if locomotion is induced. Measures nausea, disorientation, and/or oculomotor issues while using VR.
- **Link:** https://www.mdpi.com/2813-2084/2/1/2
- **Items:** 6
- **Reliability (α):** .87

**Items:**
*Rate each item from 1 to 7: (1 = Absent, 2 = Very slight, 3 = Slight, 4 = Moderate, 5 = Intense, 6 = Very intense, 7 = Extreme)*

1. **Nausea A:** Do you feel nauseous?
2. **Nausea B:** Do you feel dizzy?
3. **Vestibular A:** Do you feel disoriented?
4. **Vestibular B:** Do you feel postural instability / loss of balance?
5. **Oculomotor A:** Do you feel visually induced fatigue?
6. **Oculomotor B:** Do you feel visually induced discomfort?

**Scoring:**
- **Nausea** = Nausea A + Nausea B (range 0–14)
- **Vestibular** = Vestibular A + Vestibular B (range 0–14)
- **Oculomotor** = Oculomotor A + Oculomotor B (range 0–14)
- **Total** = Nausea + Vestibular + Oculomotor (range 0–42)

## If Participants Have a Virtual Body:

When participants have a virtual body, it is important to measure body ownership and agency. This can be done using the Alpha IVBO or the VEQ:

### 6. Illusion of Virtual Body Ownership (Alpha IVBO)
- **Purpose:** Used if participants have a virtual body. Measures the feeling that the virtual body belongs to oneself.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/VR.2015.7223379
- **Items:** 13 (Alpha IVBO Scale)

**Instructions:**
"Please answer according to your gut feeling, spontaneously and intuitively." 
*(Rated on a 7-point Likert scale: 1 = Strongly disagree; 7 = Strongly agree)*

**Items:**  
*ACCEPTANCE subscale:*  
1. [myBody] I felt as if the body I saw in the virtual mirror might be my body.  
2. [myBodyParts] I felt as if the body parts I looked upon were my body parts.  
3. [humanness] The virtual body I saw was human-like.  

*CONTROL subscale:*  
1. [myMovement] The movements I saw in the virtual mirror seemed to be my own movements.  
2. [bodyControlEnjoyment] I enjoyed controlling the virtual body I saw in the virtual mirror.  
3. [controlMovements] I felt as if I was controlling the movement I saw in the virtual mirror.  
4. [causeMovements] I felt as if I was causing the movement I saw in the virtual mirror.  

*CHANGE subscale:*  
1. [ownOtherBody] The illusion of owning a different body than my real one was very strong.  
2. [myBodyChange] At a time during the experiment I felt as if my real body changed in shape and/or texture.  
3. [myBodyCheck] During or after the task, I felt the need to check that my body still looks as expected.  
4. [echoHeavyLight] I felt an after-effect as if my body had become lighter/heavier.  
5. [echoTallSmall] I felt an after-effect as if my body had become taller/smaller.  
6. [echoLargeThin] I felt an after-effect as if my body had become larger/thinner.  

**Scoring:**  
Calculate the mean for each subscale (Acceptance, Control, Change).  

### 7. Virtual Embodiment Questionnaire (VEQ)
- **Citation:** Roth & Latoschik (2020)
- **Purpose:** Used if participants have a virtual body. Measures aspects of virtual embodiment.
- **Link:** https://sites.google.com/view/virtualembodimentquestionnaire/
- **Items:** 12
- **Reliability (α):** .88

**Items:**  
*Rated on a 7-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 7 = strongly agree)*  

*Ownership subscale:*  
1. (VEQ1) It felt like the virtual body was my body.  
2. (VEQ2) It felt like the virtual body parts were my body parts.  
3. (VEQ3) The virtual body felt like a human body.  
4. (VEQ4) It felt like the virtual body belonged to me.  

*Agency subscale:*  
1. (VEQ5) The movements of the virtual body felt like they were my movements.  
2. (VEQ6) I felt like I was controlling the movements of the virtual body.  
3. (VEQ7) I felt like I was causing the movements of the virtual body.  
4. (VEQ8) The movements of the virtual body were in sync with my own movements.  

*Change subscale:*  
1. (VEQ9) I felt like the form or appearance of my own body had changed.  
2. (VEQ10) I felt like the weight of my own body had changed.  
3. (VEQ11) I felt like the size (height) of my own body had changed.  
4. (VEQ12) I felt like the width of my own body had changed.  

**Scoring:**  
Calculate the mean per subscale (Ownership, Agency, Change). Higher scores indicate a stronger sense of embodiment.

## If Virtual Humanoids are Present:

When virtual humanoids are present, it is important to measure social presence. Co-presence and social presence are often used interchangeably, but they are distinct concepts. Co-presence is the feeling of being physically present with another humanoid entity in the virtual environment, while social presence is the feeling of being able to interact with another humanoid entity in the virtual environment.

### 8. Co-Presence & Social Presence in VEs
- **Citation:** Poeschl & Doering (2015)
- **Purpose:** Used if virtual humanoids are present. Measures the feeling of being physically present with another humanoid entity. (Designed for VR fear of public speaking / VRET applications).
- **Link:** https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Nicola-Doering/publication/291517481_Measuring_Co-Presence_and_Social_Presence_in_Virtual_Environments_-_Psychometric_Construction_of_a_German_Scale_for_a_Fear_of_Public_Speaking_Scenario/links/665391540b0d2845745bd40f/Measuring-Co-Presence-and-Social-Presence-in-Virtual-Environments-Psychometric-Construction-of-a-German-Scale-for-a-Fear-of-Public-Speaking-Scenario.pdf
- **Items:** 15
- **Reliability (α):** Factor 1 (.85), Factor 2 (.81), Factor 3 (.80), Factor 4 (.71)

**Items:**
*Rated on a 5-point Likert scale: 1 = strongly disagree → 5 = strongly agree*

*Factor 1: Presenter’s Reaction to Virtual Agents*  
1. The people’s behavior influenced my style of presentation.  
2. The people’s behavior had an influence on my mood.  
3. I reacted to the people’s behavior.  
4. I was easily distracted by the people.  

*Factor 2: Perceived Virtual Agents’ Reaction*  
1. Sometimes the people were influenced by my mood.  
2. Sometimes the people were influenced by my style of presentation.  
3. The people reacted to my actions.  
4. I was able to interpret the people’s reactions.  

*Factor 3: Impression of Interaction Possibilities*  
1. I had the feeling to interact with other human beings.  
2. I felt connected to the other people.  
3. I had the feeling that I was able to interact with people in the virtual room.  
4. I had the impression that the audience noticed me in the virtual room.  

*Factor 4: (Co-)Presence of Other People*  
1. I was aware that other people were with me in the virtual room.  
2. I had the feeling that I perceived other people in the virtual room.  
3. I felt alone in the virtual environment. (R)  

**Scoring:**
Item 15 is reverse-coded (R). Calculate the mean for each factor.

When virtual humanoids are present, it is also important to measure perceived humanness and affinity. This can be done using the following scales:


### 9. Multimodal Presence Scale (MPS)
- **Citation:** Makransky, Lilleholt, & Aaby (2017)
- **Purpose:** Measures a combination of Physical, Social, and Self-Presence based on Lee’s (2004) unified theory of presence.
- **Link:** http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2017.02.066
- **Items:** 15
- **Reliability (α):** Physical (.84–.86), Social (.83–.90), Self-Presence (.93–.94)

**Items:**
*Rated on a 5-point Likert scale: 1 = completely disagree → 5 = strongly agree*

*Physical Presence:*
1. (PHYS_2) The virtual environment seemed real to me.
2. (PHYS_3) I had a sense of acting in the virtual environment, rather than operating something from outside.
3. (PHYS_4) My experience in the virtual environment seemed consistent with my experiences in the real world.
4. (PHYS_5) While I was in the virtual environment, I had a sense of “being there”.
5. (PHYS_10) I was completely captivated by the virtual world.

*Social Presence:*
1. (SOC_1) I felt like I was in the presence of another person in the virtual environment.
2. (SOC_2) I felt that the people in the virtual environment were aware of my presence.
3. (SOC_3) The people in the virtual environment appeared to be sentient (conscious and alive) to me.
4. (SOC_5) During the simulation there were times where the computer interface seemed to disappear, and I felt like I was working directly with another person.
5. (SOC_7) I had a sense that I was interacting with other people in the virtual environment, rather than a computer simulation.

*Self-Presence:*
1. (SELF_2) I felt like my virtual embodiment was an extension of my real body within the virtual environment.
2. (SELF_3) When something happened to my virtual embodiment, it felt like it was happening to my real body.
3. (SELF_4) I felt like my real arm was projected into the virtual environment through my virtual embodiment.
4. (SELF_6) I felt like my real hand was inside of the virtual environment.
5. (SELF_7) During the simulation, I felt like my virtual embodiment and my real body became one and the same.

**Scoring:**
Items use the term “virtual embodiment” rather than “avatar” to be applicable across simulations where users do not create a custom avatar. Calculate the mean for each subscale.

Beyond co-presence and social presence, it is also important to measure perceived humanness and affinity with the virtual character. This can be done using the following scale:

### 10. Perceived Humanness and Affinity
- **Purpose:** Used if using animated avatars to measure perceptions of humanness and affinity with the virtual character.
- **Link:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4428060/
- **Items:** 13 (8 items for Perceived Humanness, 5 items for Affinity)
- **Reliability (α):** Perceived Humanness (Pilot 1: .90, Pilot 2: .92); Affinity (Pilot 1: .87, Pilot 2: .90)

**Items:**  
*Perceived Humanness:*  
1. This character has intention.  
2. This character experiences emotions.  
3. This character has free will.  
4. This character has a mind of its own.  
5. This character has consciousness.  
6. This character is lifelike.  
7. This character looks like a real person.  
8. I think this character would interact like a person.  

*Affinity:*  
1. I have an affinity with the character.     
2. I feel a likeness with the character.  
3. I feel rapport with the character.  
4. The character in the image is friendly.  
5. I feel affinity with the character in the image.  

**Scoring:**
Calculate the mean score for each construct (Perceived Humanness and Affinity).


### 11. Uncanny Valley Indices (Ho & MacDorman)
- **Purpose:** Used to evaluate animated avatars and robots across perceived humanness, eeriness, attractiveness, and warmth, serving as an alternative to the Godspeed Indices.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2010.05.015
- **Items:** 28 (including Pleasure index)

**Items:**  
*Rated via semantic differential scales:*

*1. Perceived Humanness:*    
1. Artificial – Natural  
2. Synthetic – Real  
3. Inanimate – Living  
4. Human-made – Humanlike  
5. Mechanical movement – Biological movement  
6. Without definite lifespan – Mortal

*2. Eeriness:*
*Eerie subfactor:*
1. Reassuring – Eerie
2. Numbing – Freaky
3. Ordinary – Supernatural

*Spine-tingling subfactor:*
1. Uninspiring – Spine-tingling
2. Boring – Shocking
3. Predictable – Thrilling
4. Bland – Uncanny
5. Unemotional – Hair-raising

*3. Attractiveness:*
1. Unattractive – Attractive
2. Ugly – Beautiful
3. Repulsive – Agreeable
4. Crude – Stylish
5. Messy – Sleek

*4. Warmth:*
1. Cold-hearted – Warm-hearted
2. Hostile – Friendly
3. Spiteful – Well-intentioned
4. Ill-tempered – Good-natured
5. Grumpy – Cheerful

*Other items mentioned in testing (Pleasure index):*
1. Sad – Happy
2. Bad – Good
3. Terrible – Wonderful
4. Annoyed – Pleased

**Scoring:**
Participants rate their response between the semantic differential word pairs (typically 1 to 7 or 1 to 5). Calculate the mean for each construct/subfactor.

## If Participants Interact with Virtual Objects:

When participants interact with virtual objects, it is important to measure system usability. This can be done using the following scale:

### 12. System Usability Scale (SUS)
- **Purpose:** Used if participants interact with virtual objects. Measures the perceived ease of use.
- **Link:** https://measuringu.com/sus/
- **Items:** 10
- **Reliability (α):** .91

**Items:**
1. I think that I would like to use this system frequently.
2. I found the system unnecessarily complex.
3. I thought the system was easy to use.
4. I think that I would need the support of a technical person to be able to use this system.
5. I found the various functions in this system were well integrated.
6. I thought there was too much inconsistency in this system.
7. I would imagine that most people would learn to use this system very quickly.
8. I found the system very cumbersome to use.
9. I felt very confident using the system.
10. I needed to learn a lot of things before I could get going with this system.

**Scoring:**
Standard SUS scoring (0-100 scale based on alternating positive/negative item weights).


### 13. VR System Usability Questionnaire (VRSUQ)
- **Citation:** Kim & Rhiu (2024)
- **Purpose:** Measures UX / Perceived Usability specifically for VR systems.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apergo.2024.104319
- **Items:** 9
- **Reliability (α):** .752–.773

**Items:**
*Rated on a 5-point Likert scale: 1 = strongly disagree → 5 = strongly agree*

*Effectiveness — Compatibility, Feedback, Error Prevention:*
1. The system responded well to my manipulations as expected with no delays.
2. I think the virtual reality system provides clear feedback on my manipulations.
3. I kept making errors/mistakes while using the virtual reality system. (R)

*Efficiency — Understandability, Learnability, Error Recovery:*
1. I could clearly understand the information presented within the virtual environment.
2. I think this system is user-friendly, straightforward to learn, and designed in a way that most people will find it easy to adapt to.
3. I think it is easy to correct errors made during virtual reality experiences.

*Satisfaction — Pleasure, Physical Discomfort, Emotional Discomfort:*
1. I enjoyed the virtual reality experience.
2. I felt dizzy, motion sickness, or a headache while experiencing virtual reality. (R)
3. While experiencing virtual reality, I felt mental burdens such as tension, frustration, and time pressure. (R)

**Scoring:**
Reverse coded items (R). VRSUQ score formula (corresponding to SUS 0–100 scale): `(SUM(all 9 items after recoding) / 9 - 1) * 100 / 4`.
